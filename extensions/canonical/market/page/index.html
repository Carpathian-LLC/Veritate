white line = actual price green shade = primary model went LONG here red shade = went SHORT here balance line: green where it is rising, red where falling
The white line is what the price actually did. Each colored line is one model's guess: it starts at the same price, then steps forward bar by bar following only that model's own up or down calls. A colored line that hugs the white one called direction well; one that wanders off did not. The shading and balance line above come from the primary model trading at the current boldness (set just below): green where it is long, red where it is short. The balance line on the right $ axis is colored by trend, green where the account is rising and red where it is falling. Make it bolder and more bars get shaded and the balance line redraws live.

How good were its calls

What this shows. One scorecard for the analytics model over the window you scored. Each card grades one trait of its calls: how often it is right, whether being confident actually means being correct, and whether it guesses the size of the move. Green beats a coin flip / the naive baseline; red is worse.
At short bar sizes, calling up vs down is close to a coin flip for any model, so raw accuracy is not the story. What matters: is it right more often when it is confident, and does it guess the size of the move. The cards above turn the same calls into trader terms: win rate, profit factor, expectancy and drawdown (all before fees).
