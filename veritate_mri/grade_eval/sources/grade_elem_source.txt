The summer Sara turned ten was the summer her grandmother taught her how to plant tomatoes. The garden behind Grandma Bea's small white house had been mostly empty for years, ever since Grandpa Jack passed away and Grandma stopped having the energy for it. But this year, when Sara came to stay for the long July weeks while her parents worked, Grandma decided it was time to bring the garden back.

"You're old enough now to help me do it right," Grandma said on the first morning, handing Sara a pair of work gloves that were slightly too big. "We'll start small. Just tomatoes, peppers, and maybe some basil if we're feeling brave."

Sara had never worn work gloves before. She wiggled her fingers inside them and tried to look serious, the way she imagined a real gardener would look. Grandma was already kneeling at the edge of the garden bed, prying weeds out of the dry brown soil with a rusty metal trowel.

The first thing they had to do was clear the bed. Sara was surprised by how much work this turned out to be. The weeds had grown deep roots over the years, and some of them came out only after she pulled with all her strength, falling backward when they finally let go. After about an hour, both of them were sweaty and streaked with dirt, but the bed was empty and ready.

"Now we add the good stuff," Grandma said, pointing at a bag of compost that was almost as tall as Sara. "Soil that's been sitting unused gets tired, just like people. We have to feed it."

They dumped the compost into the bed and mixed it with the older soil using shovels. Sara liked the smell. It was earthy and rich and reminded her of the woods after a long rain. She told Grandma so, and Grandma laughed and said that was exactly what good soil was supposed to smell like.

After lunch, which was tomato sandwiches even though they did not have tomatoes from the garden yet, they began planting. Grandma showed Sara how to dig a hole that was just deep enough for the roots, no deeper. She showed her how to set the small plant gently in the hole, holding it by the leaves rather than the stem because, as Grandma explained, a plant could grow new leaves but a broken stem usually meant the end of the whole thing.

By the time they finished, six tomato plants and four pepper plants stood in two neat rows. Sara stepped back to admire the work, and she realized she felt prouder of this than of anything she had done in school all year. It was a strange feeling, like the satisfaction was running through her arms and into her hands.

"Tomorrow we'll water them in the morning before the sun gets too high," Grandma said. "That's a rule. You water early, never in the heat of the day, because the roots can burn if the water sits on them in the sun."

Over the next several weeks, Sara learned more rules than she could have imagined. There were rules about pinching the suckers off the tomato plants so they would put their energy into the fruit instead of growing more leaves. There were rules about checking under the leaves for green worms that could eat a whole plant in a single afternoon. There were rules about how often to water, how to tell when a pepper was ripe, and what to do if a sudden storm threatened to flatten everything.

The first ripe tomato came in the middle of August. Sara spotted it during her morning watering, a single red fruit hidden behind a curtain of green leaves, and she shouted for Grandma to come see. Grandma came out of the kitchen still holding a spatula. Together they cut the tomato off the vine with kitchen scissors, and Sara carried it back inside as if it were made of glass.

"We earned this one," Grandma said, slicing it on a wooden cutting board. The juice ran out in a slow trickle. They each took a slice, sprinkled it with a tiny bit of salt, and ate it standing up in the kitchen.

It was the best thing Sara had ever tasted, though she could not have said exactly why. Maybe it was because she had pulled out the weeds with her own hands. Maybe it was because she had watched the plant grow from a small green sprout into something that could feed two people. Or maybe, she thought, it was just that things you worked for tasted better than things that came easily.

When her parents came to pick her up at the end of August, Sara's hands were tan and a little rough, and there was a small scar on her thumb from where a thorn from the rose bush had caught her one morning. She brought home a paper bag full of tomatoes and peppers, more than her family could eat in a week.

"You did all of this?" her mother asked, holding up a bright red tomato that was almost as big as her fist.

"Grandma and I did it together," Sara said. "But she said next summer I can plan the garden myself if I want. She said I'm a real gardener now."

She thought about that on the long drive home, watching the green fields slide past the car window. A real gardener. It was a small thing, maybe, but it felt very large. She closed her eyes for a moment and could still smell the soil on her hands, and the slight tang of the tomato vines when you brushed against them in the early morning.
