The persistent difficulty of articulating a coherent theory of historical causation owes less to a paucity of available frameworks than to the irreducibly contested nature of the entities upon which any such theory must necessarily operate. Whether one approaches the problem through the lens of structural determination, methodological individualism, or some hybrid of the two, the basic question remains stubbornly recurrent: what are the units whose interactions are taken to constitute historical change, and by what criteria are those units to be individuated from the dense, undifferentiated flux in which they appear to be embedded? Recent scholarship in the analytic tradition has tended to retreat from grand systematic claims in favor of more modest, domain-specific theorizing, a retreat that is defensible insofar as it acknowledges the genuine epistemic limits imposed by historical distance and the chronic scarcity of relevant evidence, but that has also produced, as its inadvertent corollary, a fragmentation of historiographical discourse that makes cross-disciplinary synthesis increasingly difficult to achieve.

One can trace this fragmentation, in part, to the methodological commitments inherited from the social sciences during the middle decades of the twentieth century, when the ascendency of quantitative methods and the institutional consolidation of the academic disciplines produced a tacit division of intellectual labor that was rarely subjected to systematic justification. Sociologists were tasked with the explanation of contemporary collective behavior; economists with the modeling of resource allocation under conditions of relative scarcity; political scientists with the formal analysis of institutional dynamics; and historians, having been left with what remained, were charged with documenting the past in whatever idiom seemed most plausible to the prevailing intellectual fashion of the moment. The boundaries between these disciplines were never as crisp as their institutional architecture suggested, of course, but the practical effect of the division was to discourage the kind of synoptic theorizing that had characterized the work of nineteenth-century thinkers from Marx to Tocqueville.

It is instructive, in this context, to consider the way the question of agency has been displaced rather than resolved by successive theoretical movements. Structuralist accounts in the mid-century tradition, to take only the most prominent example, tended to treat individual actors as the carriers of impersonal social forces whose logic was held to be analytically prior to any expression in particular biographies; the consequence was that the agents themselves became theoretically transparent, mere conduits through which the underlying structures asserted themselves. The reaction against this position, when it came, took the form of a renewed interest in the experiential and discursive dimensions of historical actors, but the resulting body of work, rich as it has often been in textured detail, has rarely engaged with the structural conditions that the previous generation had taken to be its principal subject matter, with the result that the two literatures continue to develop in something like parallel ignorance of one another.

What is needed, it seems, is not the abandonment of structural analysis nor the dismissal of agency-centered work, but rather a sustained methodological reflection on the relationship between the two, conducted with sufficient rigor to permit specific, testable claims about which structural features condition which classes of action under which historical circumstances. This is, of course, easier to demand than to produce, and the existing attempts in this direction, from the various theories of practice in the late twentieth century to the more recent revival of interest in causal mechanisms, have generally fallen short of their own ambitions in one respect or another. Nevertheless, the alternative, which is to continue to produce distinguished but mutually unintelligible bodies of scholarship, is unsatisfying both intellectually and practically, particularly at a moment in which the public demand for historical understanding has rarely been more acute.

A useful starting point would be to take seriously the methodological insight that the relevant units of analysis are not given in advance by the data but are themselves theoretical constructs whose adequacy must be assessed against the explanatory ambitions of the inquiry. The category of "the working class," to take a familiar instance, may be perfectly serviceable for one set of questions and quite misleading for another, and the researcher's task is not to discover, by some procedure of careful observation, the true membership of the category but rather to specify, in advance, the analytic role the category is expected to play and to evaluate its performance against that specification. Categories that have been smuggled in from earlier theoretical traditions without such reassessment are precisely the ones most likely to produce the appearance of explanation while contributing very little to genuine understanding.

The implications of this position are not modest. It requires, among other things, a willingness to revise long-established taxonomies when the evidence suggests they have outlived their utility, and a corresponding willingness to acknowledge that the resulting theoretical vocabulary will itself be provisional, subject to further revision as new questions are pressed and new evidence emerges. Such a stance is uncomfortable for a discipline that has traditionally drawn part of its authority from the apparent stability of its central concepts, but the discomfort is the unavoidable cost of an intellectual practice that takes seriously its own status as an inquiry rather than a body of doctrine. Any other arrangement risks confusing the conventions of the field with the structure of the world it claims to describe, and that confusion, however well institutionalized, is the surest form of methodological complacency.
