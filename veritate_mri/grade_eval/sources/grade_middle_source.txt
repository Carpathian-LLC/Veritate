Twelve was the age when Eli finally decided he was old enough to know the truth about the house at the end of Maple Street, and he wasn't going to let his older sister Cara talk him out of it any longer. Every kid at his school had a different story about the place, ranging from the merely plausible (an elderly recluse lived there alone) to the wildly improbable (it was haunted by the ghost of someone's great-aunt who had vanished without a trace in 1962). Eli, who considered himself a careful thinker, had decided that the only way to settle the question was to walk up to the front door and knock.

He waited until a Saturday afternoon when his parents were at the farmer's market and Cara was over at her best friend's house pretending to study. The October air was crisp and slightly damp, and the sidewalk was already covered with a thin layer of yellow leaves that crunched under his sneakers as he walked. Maple Street had only twelve houses on it, six on each side, and the one at the end was set back from the road behind a low stone wall and a row of overgrown hedges.

As he got closer, he started to feel the kind of nervousness that pretends to be excitement until you stop and notice what it actually is. His heart was beating faster than it should have been for a simple walk down the block. He tried to remind himself that there was nothing dangerous about knocking on a door, and that the worst thing that could happen was that no one would answer, but the reminder did not really help.

The gate at the front of the property was made of black iron, and when he pushed it open, it let out a long, complaining squeak that he was sure could be heard halfway down the street. A flagstone path led to the front porch, with weeds growing up between the stones in irregular tufts. The porch itself was wide and shaded, with two empty rocking chairs that faced each other as if waiting for a conversation that had not happened in a long time.

Eli took a deep breath and knocked. Three quick knocks, the way his father always did when they visited his grandmother. He listened. There was a long silence, long enough that he began to relax, thinking he could now go back home and tell himself he had at least tried. But then he heard footsteps coming closer, and the door opened just a crack, and a single eye peered out at him from the dim hallway beyond.

"Yes?" said a voice that was older than Eli had expected, but also less frightening. It sounded tired more than anything else.

"Hi," Eli said, suddenly aware that he had not actually planned what he was going to say. "I'm Eli. I live a few houses down. I, um, just wanted to introduce myself. People at school say a lot of things about this house, and I thought it wasn't fair to keep believing them without ever meeting you."

There was a pause. Then the door opened wider, and Eli saw a man who must have been in his seventies, with white hair and a soft, slightly rumpled cardigan. The man's face was lined with years that did not look unhappy, only quiet, the way the faces of people who have spent a long time alone can sometimes look.

"Well," the man said, with what might have been the beginning of a smile. "That is one of the more honest reasons anyone has ever given me for ringing my bell. I am Mr. Hadlow. Would you like to come in and have some tea, or would you prefer to do this on the porch?"

Eli, who had not actually planned to come inside any house belonging to a stranger, hesitated for a moment and chose the porch. They sat in the two rocking chairs that had earlier looked so empty, and Mr. Hadlow brought out a kettle and two mismatched mugs and a small plate of cookies that were slightly stale but not unpleasant. As they sipped their tea and the autumn afternoon stretched golden across the unkempt lawn, Mr. Hadlow began, slowly and without any drama, to tell Eli about himself.

He had taught history at the local high school for almost forty years, until a small stroke a decade ago had made standing in front of a class for that long no longer practical. His wife had died three years before that, and they had never had children, so when he retired, he had simply stopped seeing most people. It was not that he wanted to be alone, he explained. It was just that the habits of being among others had quietly atrophied, and after a while it had begun to feel like too much effort to start them up again.

"You should know," he added, "that I am not haunted. I am not even particularly mysterious. I am only a man who got into the habit of being quiet, and stayed there longer than he probably should have."

Eli nodded, trying to imagine what it would be like to live in a house for that many years without anyone really knowing what was inside. It struck him, sitting there with his now-warm mug, that all the wild stories had been the neighborhood's way of filling in a silence with something more interesting than the actual truth, which was just that one tired old man had grown invisible to people who never bothered to look.

When Eli finally stood to leave, Mr. Hadlow walked him to the gate and shook his hand. "Come back anytime," he said. "I will not promise that I will be exciting, but I will promise that I will be polite."

Walking home in the lengthening shadows of the late afternoon, Eli thought he had probably not learned anything that any of the kids at school would consider especially thrilling. But he also thought he had learned something more useful, which was that a person could be a stranger for a very long time, and then suddenly not be one, and the only thing it took was for someone to bother to ask.
