VERITATE RESEARCH ARTICLES: INDEX

A series of academic-style articles on the Veritate project, a research program
building byte-level, energy-efficient language models that train and run on
consumer hardware (a single Apple M3 Ultra). Every quantitative claim traces to
the project's internal research log; general scientific claims are cited to the
public literature.

01_byte_level_modeling.txt
   Why Veritate uses a 256-symbol byte vocabulary with no tokenizer, the
   sequence-length context tax it costs, and what the measured per-byte entropy
   distribution and small-scale failure modes tell us about the trade.

02_data_efficiency.txt
   Measured throughput sweeps showing the M3 Ultra is bandwidth-bound, not
   framework-bound (PyTorch versus an MLX port), and why data quality and the
   training objective, not framework speed, are the real levers for small-model
   quality.

03_quantization_aware_training.txt
   The measured cliff: below four bits per weight, post-training quantization on
   byte models is catastrophic, while quantization-aware training recovers nearly
   all the quality, reaching an effective 1.98 bits per weight at small quality
   cost.

04_activation_sparsity.txt
   Why activation sparsity cannot be retrofitted onto a trained model but can be
   installed with a few minutes of fine-tuning, reaching about 88 percent zeros,
   and how it composes with weight pruning to cut feed-forward compute to roughly
   six percent of dense.

05_multitoken_speculative_decode.txt
   Lossless decode acceleration at byte level: measured internal agreement in the
   published acceptance band, a 19-times byte-exact key-value-cache speedup, and
   self-speculative drafting that composes to a 2.25-times theoretical speedup
   with unchanged output.

06_test_time_compute.txt
   Trading parameter count for thinking time: the complexity-theory frame, public
   evidence from small reasoners, and Veritate's own measurement that best-of-N
   self-grading and adaptive-depth compute savings appear even at 85M.
